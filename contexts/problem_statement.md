Problem statement

Aircraft defect assessment and maintenance decision-making still depend heavily on manual review of technical documents and engineer experience.

Maintenance Control Center and engineering teams must continuously reference large volumes of documentation, including AMM, MEL, CDL, TSM, engineering orders, and historical defect records.

As fleets grow and operational pressure increases, manual validation becomes more time-consuming and more susceptible to human error.

Current challenges

Engineers spend significant time searching and cross-referencing maintenance documentation.

Incorrect references to AMM tasks or MEL items may lead to delays or operational risk.

Defect classifications and dispatch conditions are manually entered and prone to inconsistencies.

Knowledge is fragmented across multiple repositories and historical records.

Fatigue and workload increase the probability of missed information.

Pilot scope

Retrieve relevant maintenance procedures from technical manuals.

Validate defect entries against approved maintenance documentation.

Recommend relevant AMM, MEL, CDL, or TSM references.

Highlight potential inconsistencies before engineer submission.

Provide explainable reasoning and source references.

Available data

Aircraft Maintenance Manual (AMM).

Minimum Equipment List (MEL).

Configuration Deviation List (CDL).

Troubleshooting Manual (TSM).

Historical defect logs, engineering procedures, and maintenance records, subject to approval.

What startups can provide

Generative AI.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG).

Document Intelligence.

Knowledge Graphs.

Maintenance Decision Support Systems.

Build direction

Build an AI-powered maintenance copilot that retrieves approved procedures, validates defect entries, recommends AMM/MEL/CDL/TSM references, flags inconsistencies, and explains its reasoning with source references.

What makes a strong submission?
A clear one-line problem statement, a working live demo (not just slides), and an accurate list of every AI tool, model, and partner credit you used.

The AABW event portal. Devpost is no longer used for this event.

Can I submit the same project to more than one track?
Yes, if it’s genuinely relevant to each track. It’s not recommended to enter a track your project doesn’t truly fit.

What’s the most common submission mistake?
Leaving required fields blank or entering inaccurate information — description, team list, tools used, and track selection all need to be complete and correct.

Do I need to be there in person?
Yes. At least one team representative must check in on-site on Jul 12 to confirm your team is pitching.

Why does it matter which tools I record?
Several tech partners run bonus prizes on top of track prizes — recording and correctly selecting the tools you used is what makes you eligible for those.

Can we get real data from our enterprise partner?
We’re chasing it, but if it isn’t in hand by end of day today, it’s probably not coming. Plan to build with synthetic data instead. If your idea can’t work with synthetic data, drop that part of the idea rather than wait on the data.

BEYOND THE CHECKLIST

Remaining Best Practices

1. State the problem in one sentence before the solution. Make your Problem/Track Fit unmissable from the first line of your pitch.

2. Build for the actual enterprise brief, not a hypothetical. AABW tracks are grounded in real problem statements from partners like AWS, Vietjet, Tasco, and KFC Vietnam — solve the brief that’s given.

3. Record a backup demo video. Wifi and API hiccups happen inside your pitch window — a fallback protects your Technical Execution.

4. Record every tool and tech partner you use — accurately. Several tech partners run bonus prizes on top of track prizes, so select your tools correctly in the submission portal to be considered.

5. Disclose any pre-existing code or assets. Using something you built before AABW is fine — not disclosing it is not.

6. Assign one team member to own the pitch. A single confident narrator communicates more clearly than a group hand-off.

7. Treat Demo Day as a pilot conversation. Enterprise partners are in the room to find teams to work with after judging — not just to hand out trophies.
